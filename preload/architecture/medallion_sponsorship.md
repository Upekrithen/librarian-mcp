# Pudding #76: The Medallion

*What if sponsoring someone was not charity — it was structure?*

---

## At a Glance

You believe in a leather crafter's work. You want to support them — not with a donation, not with a tip, but with something that lasts. You buy a Medallion Sponsorship: a one-time commitment that gives the crafter a boost in Credits and visibility, with your name attached as a sponsor.

This is not patronage. It is not investment. It is structured support — one level, one sponsor, one relationship. No pyramid. No MLM. No second-degree attribution.

---

## More Info

### The Sponsorship Gap

Existing platforms offer two ways to support creators:

**Tips/donations.** One-time gifts with no structure. The creator gets $5. The relationship ends. No tracking, no ongoing connection, no mutual benefit.

**Subscriptions.** Recurring payments for access to content. The creator produces, the subscriber consumes. It is a transaction, not a partnership.

Neither model creates a *relationship* between supporter and creator. The tipper disappears. The subscriber is anonymous. The creator does not know who believes in them — only who paid.

### The Medallion Sponsorship

A Medallion Sponsorship is a formal, public act of support. When you sponsor a creator:

1. **You commit Credits.** A defined amount, paid once. Not recurring. Not a subscription.
2. **The creator receives the Credits** plus a visibility boost — their project is featured in the sponsor's network.
3. **Your name is attached.** Not hidden. Not anonymous. You are publicly listed as a sponsor of this creator. Your reputation is linked to their success.
4. **Attribution is one level.** You sponsored them. If someone they interact with later sponsors someone else, you get no credit for that. One level. Period. This is the anti-MLM rule.

### Why One Level Matters

Multi-level attribution is the mechanism that turns legitimate support into predatory schemes. "Sign up three people, they each sign up three people, and you earn from all of them." This is how MLMs extract value from the bottom of the pyramid.

The Medallion Sponsorship kills this at the design level. You sponsor Maria. Maria later sponsors James. You get nothing from Maria's sponsorship of James. Zero. The chain breaks at one.

This means the only reason to sponsor someone is because you believe in their work — not because you hope to profit from a downstream chain. The incentive is clean because the structure is clean.

## Full Detail

### What the Sponsor Gets

Sponsorship is not charity — the sponsor receives real cooperative benefits:

- **Sponsorship Marks.** You earn Marks for sponsoring — recognition of your contribution to the cooperative's growth.
- **Public attribution.** Your name on the creator's project page as a sponsor. This builds your own reputation.
- **Sponsor network effects.** Other members who see your sponsorship may explore your own projects, creating organic cross-pollination.
- **The satisfaction of structure.** You know exactly where your Credits went, who received them, and what they were used for. This is not a black-box donation. It is a transparent investment in a specific person's work.

### What the Creator Gets

- **Credits.** Immediate, usable platform service tokens.
- **Visibility boost.** Featured in the sponsor's feed and network.
- **Social proof.** "This creator has 7 sponsors" tells other members that real people believe in this work.
- **No obligations.** The creator does not owe the sponsor anything. No deliverables, no reports, no equity. The sponsorship is a gift with attribution — not a contract.

### Medallion Sponsorship and Cue Cards

The Cue Card system and Medallion Sponsorship are designed to work together. A member who shares a Cue Card and generates a new member signup earns one-level attribution. A member who sponsors a creator through a Medallion earns one-level attribution.

In both cases: one level. One sponsor. One relationship. No chains. No trees. No pyramids.

This consistency is deliberate. Whether you are spreading the word (Cue Cards) or putting Credits behind someone's work (Medallions), the attribution model is identical. The cooperative has one rule for how support is recognized — and that rule has one level.

### The Economics of Generosity

A Medallion Sponsorship moves Credits from one member's account to another's. The cooperative does not take a cut. There is no "sponsorship fee." The full amount goes to the creator.

Wait — doesn't the cooperative always take Cost+20%? Not on Medallions. The sponsorship is a peer-to-peer Credit transfer (the only kind the cooperative allows), and the cooperative treats it as a governance action, not a commercial transaction. The cooperative facilitates. It does not extract.

This exception is structural. Extracting a margin from acts of generosity would undermine the cooperative's culture. The whole point of the Medallion is: you believe in someone's work enough to put your own Credits behind it. The cooperative should make that easier, not more expensive.
